# 1. Identify Your "Pain Point" or "Spark"
Don't just build a generic calculator. Ask yourself:
## The Utility Angle: Is there a boring task you do every day that a Python script could automate? (e.g., sorting your "Downloads" folder).
## The Curiosity Angle: Is there a game or app you love? Could you build a simplified "clone" of one mechanic from it?
## The Hardware Angle: If you’re leaning toward Mechatronics, what’s a physical problem you want to solve? (e.g., a robot that avoids your cat).

# 2. The "Goldilocks" Rule (Scope)
The biggest project killer is picking something too big.
##Too Hard: Making a 3D Multiplayer RPG.
##Too Easy: Making a "Hello World" script.
##Just Right: A project where you know how to do 60% of it, but have to research the other 40%.
3. Choose Your Stack Based on Interest
Match your project idea to one of the pathways you mentioned:
Software (Python): Build a Discord bot, a simple weather app, or a text-based adventure game.
Mechatronics (Maqueen): Create a robot that follows a line or uses sensors to play "tag" with another robot.
Design (Tinkercad): Design a custom enclosure for a Micro:Bit or a functional desk organiser to print. 
4. Set a "Definition of Done"
Before you write one line of code, decide what "finished" looks like. In Hack Club terms: what is the minimum viable product (MVP) you need to build to earn that loot?
